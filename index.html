Let Go
a poem's journey through five technologies of text
Origin · Before any hand has touched me

I'm a poem. I exist whole and unread, before anyone decides what I mean or what I should have said.

Let go. When your heart wears the burden of years past, and your mind clogs with emotions unexpressed, and your soul grows sick with longing for happiness, let go. Let go of the yearning wish for a different path, for you have been given a beautiful life. You can only fly if you free your wings. Free yourself from the chains of the past, from the chains tying you to an unwelcome throne. Forgive the ones that wronged you, and forgive yourself — for no one wrongs you more than you do yourself. Forgive yourself, and you will be happy.
Stage I · The Manuscript · A scribe who thinks he knows better

He copies me onto fresh vellum — slowly and carefully. But he's uncomfortable with how direct I am. So he rewrites as he goes, convinced he's just clarifying what I really meant.

He has changed several lines, thinking they would still mean the same. But they do not. He has altered the personal element within me.
Stage I · The Manuscript · A scribe who reads carefully

He copies me slowly and keeps my words, but he can't help talking back to me in the margins. He adds an instruction, a confession, and an ending that finishes my thought better than I did.

Stage II · The Printing Press · One damaged original, one clean copy

A century passes. A printer finds two things: the scribe's clean vellum copy, and the original paper manuscript — water-damaged and words missing. He has to decide. He doesn't know what you know: the clean copy already changed you.
