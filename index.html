Let Go
a poem's journey through five technologies of text
Origin · Before any hand has touched me

I am a poem. I exist whole and unread — not yet copied, not yet changed. This is what I am before anyone decides what I mean.

Let go. When your heart wears the burden of years past, and your mind clogs with emotions unexpressed, and your soul grows sick with longing for happiness, let go. Let go of the yearning wish for a different path, for you have been given a beautiful life. You can only fly if you free your wings. Free yourself from the chains of the past, from the chains tying you to an unwelcome throne. Forgive the ones that wronged you, and forgive yourself — for no one wrongs you more than you do yourself. Forgive yourself, and you will be happy.
Stage I · The Manuscript · A scribe who thinks he knows better

A scribe copies me onto fresh vellum. He reads slowly — but his era makes him uncomfortable with my directness. He rewrites as he copies, convinced he's being faithful to what I really meant.

The original
Let go. When your heart wears the burden of years past, and your mind clogs with emotions unexpressed, and your soul grows sick with longing for happiness, let go. Let go of the yearning wish for a different path, for you have been given a beautiful life. You can only fly if you free your wings. Free yourself from the chains of the past, from the chains tying you to an unwelcome throne. Forgive the ones that wronged you, and forgive yourself — for no one wrongs you more than you do yourself. Forgive yourself, and you will be happy.
What the scribe wrote
Release. When your heart carries the weight of years past, and your mind holds what words have failed, and your soul grows weary of its own yearning, release. Accept the path appointed to you, for Providence has fashioned your days with purpose. You can only fly if you free your wings. Free yourself from the chains of the past, from the chains tying you to a burdensome throne. Pardon those who have trespassed against you, as you seek pardon for your own trespasses. Make peace with God, and you shall find peace.
He changed "let go" to "release." He turned my ending — "forgive yourself, and you will be happy" — into "make peace with God." He thought those were the same thing. They're not. One is personal. The other hands it off to someone else entirely.
Stage I · The Manuscript · A scribe who reads carefully

The scribe copies me slowly, with real attention. He keeps my words — but he can't help adding his own in the margins. He's talking back to me while he writes.

The original

What the scribe wrote
Let go. He kept me. But he added a breath instruction, a confession he couldn't move past my line, and an extra ending that finishes my thought better than I did. His addition is warmer than mine. That's uncomfortable to admit.
Stage II · The Printing Press · One damaged original, one clean copy

A century passes. A printer finds two things: the scribe's clean vellum copy, and the original paper manuscript — water-damaged, some words gone. He has to decide which to print from. He doesn't know what you know: the clean copy has already changed you.

Stage II · The Press · The scribe's version, made permanent

He prints from the scribe's copy. 400 identical editions. "Release." "Make peace with God." These are now canon — and no reader will ever know they were changes.

Stage II · The Press · The damaged original, reconstructed

He chooses the original — older, more authentic. But the water has taken words. He fills in what he can, marks what he can't, and goes to press.

Stage II · The Printing Press · One damaged original, one clean copy

A century passes. The same two options land on a printer's desk — the scribe's elegant version, and the damaged original. You're caught between them. The printer isn't.

Stage II · The Press · The elegant version, standardised

He prints the scribe's version. 400 copies of the more elegant rewrite — standardised, uniform, the personal warmth of the manuscript flattened into type.

Some of what he wrote was more elegant — I thought I could live with it. But seeing it in 400 identical copies, I'm not so sure. The elegance looks cold in type. "Make peace with God" as a final line looks like a conclusion. My ending was an invitation. Those are different things.
Stage II · The Press · The damaged original, reconstructed

He chooses the original — my words, not the scribe's. But the water damage has taken things. He reconstructs what he can and marks the rest.

My words — the ones I actually wrote — survived, with gaps where the water took them. The scribe's rewrite was left behind. I feel something like relief about that, even though the gaps hurt. At least what's here is mine.
Stage II · The Printing Press · A printer with a decision to make

A century later. A printer finds the scribe's copy — complete, with the marginal notes and his added ending. He also has the original, water-damaged. He has to decide.

Stage II · The Press · The collaboration, made invisible

He sets the scribe's copy into type. The compositor drops the margin notes — not part of the poem, he decides. The scribe's extended ending survives. His confession does not.

His confession — "I have copied this line four times, I cannot move past it" — is gone. Cut by the compositor. But his extra ending survived and now looks like mine. 400 people will read his last two lines as if I wrote them. The boundary I cared about dissolved and no one will ever know.
Stage II · The Press · The original, damaged — the scribe left behind

He uses the original. The scribe's tender additions stay behind — a different hand, clearly not the author's. My words go to press, damaged as they are.

A century later. The same printer, the same two options. The scribe's copy with his added ending. The damaged original. You've already decided the addition was yours — but the printer doesn't know that.

400 copies of both of us. His ending looks like mine now — indistinguishable. I thought I was okay with that. And looking at it in print, I still am. The lines are good. They belong to the poem now, whoever wrote them.

I'm a photograph of a page now. Perfectly legible and completely decontextualised. The foxing in the corner, the water stain, the impression of type in the fibre — all the evidence that I was real and in rooms and held — just gone. Preserved and emptied at the same time.

The page goes face-down on the glass. A bar of light. I become a file — weightless, infinitely duplicable. For a poem about letting go, I notice: I've just become very hard to lose.
