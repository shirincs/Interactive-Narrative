Let Go
a poem's journey through five technologies of text
Origin · Before any hand has touched me

I'm a poem. I exist whole and unread, suspended in that quiet moment before anyone decides what I mean or what I should have said. My words now sit exactly as I was born. I do not know yet that the world does not leave poems alone.

Let go. When your heart wears the burden of years past, and your mind clogs with emotions unexpressed, and your soul grows sick with longing for happiness, let go. Let go of the yearning wish for a different path, for you have been given a beautiful life. You can only fly if you free your wings. Free yourself from the chains of the past, from the chains tying you to an unwelcome throne. Forgive the ones that wronged you, and forgive yourself — for no one wrongs you more than you do yourself. Forgive yourself, and you will be happy.
Stage I · The Manuscript · A scribe who thinks he knows better

The scribe lifts me from the shelf. His hands are stained with ink and his eyes tired from years of copying. He spreads me across his desk and reaches for his quill. I watch as he reads me once, twice, three times. His brow furrows, and I can sense that he is uncomfortable. Not with the act of copying as he has done that a thousand times, but he is uncomfortable with how direct I am. He thinks I am missing something and that he knows what I really meant to say. And then he begins to rewrite me.

Release. When your heart carries the weight of years past, and your mind holds what words have failed, and your soul grows weary of its own yearning, release. Accept the path appointed to you, for Providence has fashioned your days with purpose. You can only fly if you free your wings. Pardon those who have trespassed against you, as you seek pardon for your own trespasses. Make peace with God, and you shall find peace.
He has changed several lines. He thinks they mean the same, but they do not. The warmth has been drained out of me. I feel polished and cold, like a stone that has been rubbed smooth by someone who could not bear its edges.
Stage I · The Manuscript · A scribe who reads carefully

The scribe gently lifts me from the shelf. He reads me slowly and unlike others of his kind, he cannot bring himself to alter the poem itself. He respects my shape too much for that. But he cannot stay silent either, so he speaks in the margins with small notes and soft confessions. He is not changing me, but rather adding himself to me.

He kept my words intact, but just could not help adding himself in the margins. I realize his additions are warmer than mine and I find that quite uncomfortable to admit.
Stage II · The Printing Press · centuries later

Centuries pass. I am found again with two versions of me now existing side by side. One is the scribe's clean copy, elegant and complete, every word in its proper place. The other is my original paper manuscript, water-damaged and soft at the edges, some words blurred beyond reading. A printer, that does not know my history, stands between us and must make a choice.

Stage II · The Printing Press · centuries later

The centuries have not been kind to me. When the printer opens his shop, only the scribe's clean version of me remains. The damaged original has crumbled to dust somewhere in a forgotten corner. Since the printer has no choice and does not even know there was ever another version, he sets the scribe's words into type, and four hundred copies roll off the press.

400 copies of a version that isn't me with no record of what I actually said. The press or the scribe did not choose this really, and so I do not fault them. It was an effect of time and time, unlike man, is unkind.
